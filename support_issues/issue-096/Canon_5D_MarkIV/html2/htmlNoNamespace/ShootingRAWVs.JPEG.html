Shooting RAW vs. JPEG

Shooting in RAW format enables you to produce better results if you post-process your images. Shooting in JPEG produces small files and requires no post-processing.

Shooting in RAW format has several advantages, the most important of which is no loss of image data when the file is stored, and the ability to adjust many aspects of the photo in post-processing without losing quality. For example, you can use Adobe Camera Raw or Adobe Photoshop Lightroom to adjust the white balance of a shot after the fact. RAW files are not compressed and are therefore not lossy. One disadvantage of shooting in RAW is larger file sizes, which means you can fit fewer images on your card.

Shooting in JPEG requires no post-processing. The image is processed in the camera, and attributes such as sharpness, contrast, and saturation are applied before saving the image to the card. Some cameras enable you to adjust how these attributes are applied by creating profiles.

JPEG is a compressed and therefore lossy format. However, you can control the amount of compression with camera settings. These settings in your 5D Mark IV are called Quality. Table 1 describes the various JPEG quality settings.

RAW vs. JPEG File Size

The Canon 5D Mark IV RAW file size is 24.4 MB. In contrast, a large sized JPEG is 10.2 MB, while a medium sized JPEG is only 4.2 MB
